What I Do


Organizations create knowledge every day, and the company's expertise develops through its people's experience. Yet so much of that knowledge remains surprisingly fragile across entire industries. It lives in conversations, meetings, and the minds of the people who just happen to be present.

My work focuses on preserving that knowledge before it disappears due to the natural ebb and flow of human resources.

I create documentation, knowledge bases, process guides, reference materials, and training resources that help organizations retain context, transfer expertise, and make sure that the information is accessible to the people who need it, when they need it. My goal is to preserve the context, decisions, and expertise that people will need later.



How I Got Here


Before focusing on documentation and knowledge management, I spent several years working across SaaS organizations in industries including fintech, supply chain, cybersecurity, and entertainment.

Across those environments, I noticed the same pattern repeatedly, which was that teams solved problems, developed expertise, and generated valuable institutional knowledge, but a lot of that understanding was never captured anywhere official. Then, when people left, priorities shifted, or time passed, important context disappeared with them.

Over time, I've become increasingly interested in the challenge behind the challenge: ensuring the knowledge required to solve the company's recurring problems remained available in the future.

That interest eventually led me toward documentation, knowledge management, and organizational learning.



My Approach


Preserve nuance. Good documentation should retain the context, assumptions, and implications that make knowledge useful. A document can be technically accurate while still failing to communicate what matters.

Exercise judgment. Preserving knowledge does not mean recording everything. The most valuable documentation balances completeness with usability, helping people find and understand what they need without overwhelming them.

Support action. Documentation succeeds when it enables something: faster onboarding, better decisions, fewer repeated mistakes, stronger collaboration, or more consistent execution. Knowledge becomes valuable when people can use it.
