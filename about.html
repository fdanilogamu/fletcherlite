What I Do


Organizations create knowledge every day. Decisions are made, lessons are learned, processes evolve, and expertise develops through experience. Yet much of that knowledge remains surprisingly fragile. It lives in conversations, meetings, inboxes, and the minds of the people who happen to be present.

My work focuses on preserving that knowledge before it disappears.

I create documentation, knowledge bases, process guides, reference materials, and training resources that help organizations retain context, transfer expertise, and make information accessible to the people who need it. My goal is not to document everything. It's to preserve what will matter later.



How I Got Here


Before focusing on documentation and knowledge management, I spent several years working across SaaS organizations in industries including fintech, supply chain, cybersecurity, and entertainment.

Across those environments, I noticed the same pattern repeatedly. Teams solved problems, developed expertise, and created valuable institutional knowledge, but much of that understanding was never captured. When people left, priorities shifted, or time passed, important context disappeared with them.

Over time, I became increasingly interested in the challenge behind the challenge: not just solving problems, but ensuring the knowledge required to solve them remained available in the future.

That interest eventually led me toward documentation, knowledge management, and organizational learning.



My Approach


Preserve nuance. Good documentation should retain the context, assumptions, and implications that make knowledge useful. A document can be technically accurate while still failing to communicate what matters.

Exercise judgment. Preserving knowledge does not mean recording everything. The most valuable documentation balances completeness with usability, helping people find and understand what they need without overwhelming them.

Support action. Documentation succeeds when it enables something: faster onboarding, better decisions, fewer repeated mistakes, stronger collaboration, or more consistent execution. Knowledge becomes valuable when people can use it.
